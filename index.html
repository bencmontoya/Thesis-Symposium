‘How Many Mics?’: Radical and mimetic politics of authenticity in the Digital Oral

A podcast-style thesis about the networked communities of podcast listeners and commercial rap music audiences. Together, they make up a new kind of online space that provides a unique intervention into debates of authenticity and reality through manipulation of the natural voice.

by Ben Montoya

"Soundcloud Rap and the Rise of Hyper-realistic Artistry"

April 7th, 2019

Description:

In this episode, Ben takes a look at how music streaming services and social media platforms are changing artistry and the responsibility of audiences in a click-based music economy. By taking an in-depth look at Soundcloud rap icon XXXTentacion, this episode delves into post-#MeToo discourses of accountability and its role in the underground rap communities made possible through digital infrastructure like Soundcloud. This episode also starts to interrogate the role hyper-realism plays in rap music and the Digital Oral spaces that rely on manipulation of narrative and objectivity for political potency.

Ben Vox: As both a critic and a rapper, Michael has noticed a cultural shift. With the rise of social media, the music industry has transitioned to a stream-based economy of clicks, likes and retweets. It doesn’t take much to lure someone into watching a video or checking out a song and it only benefits the artist, even if not everyone who clicks actually agrees with them.

The outlaw character in hip hop has always been seductive to listeners. The rise of gangsta rap in the 80s and 90s tapped into this counterculture trope. And people ate it up, craving an unfiltered rawness in their music. Today, social media makes it feel like artists are just a tap away, like we as the audience can truly know celebrities because we can see into their lives at any moment. Their character, their performance, is even more tied to the rest of their life because the rest of their life always has an audience. An audience that celebrates the outlaw, instead of punishing them for doing bad things.

Brooklyn rapper Tekashi 6ix9ine is another prime example of this kind of fame. 6ix9ine had spent years of branding himself through gang affiliation and gun toting Instagram videos, which landed him millions of streams and high profile feature spots. But last year, the rainbow haired, raspy voiced 22-year old was charged with gang related racketeering and weapons charges, weeks before his new album was scheduled to be released. Instead of sinking his career, the album, Dummy Boy debuted at no.2 on the Billboard 200.
